       Achrekar, who played only a solitary first-class game, rose to prominence in the 1980s when several of his students made names in Mumbai's local cricket               Legendary coach Ramakant Achrekar passed away on Wednesday (January 2), aged 87. Achrekar, born in 1932, was a massive figure in Mumbai's local cricket circuit with several of his wards going on to represent the country.                While Sachin Tendulkar was his most famous protege, a few others like Chandrakant Pandit, Sanjay Bangar, Pravin Amre and Ramesh Powar have gone on to achieve success as coaches at higher levels.                Achrekar, who has played only a solitary first-class game, rose to prominence in the 1980s when several of his students made names in Mumbai's local cricket. Apart from Tendulkar, he has coached around a dozen international cricketers, including the likes of Vinod Kambli and Ajit Agarkar who went on to play more than 100 matches for India.                Achrekar was conferred with the Dronacharya Award in 2010, becoming only the third from the field of cricket to be bestowed the honour. 20 years later, in 2010, he was awarded the Padma Shri - the fourth highest civilian honour in India.           
   'A Lion at Shivaji Park'     It's an anecdote which sums up Ramakant Achrekar, who passed away on Wednesday at the age of 87, the best.                So excited was the man upon seeing a talented youngster play tennis-ball cricket in Chembur that he landed at his house past midnight. The idea was to convince the boy's reluctant father to change his son's school so that he could focus on cricket. Realising that the father was adamant that his son would be better off studying well and taking up a job to support his middle-class family, Achrekar did something which left the father stunned and embarrassed at the same time.                "It's difficult to believe, but sir took out Rs. 1000 from his pocket - which in 1978 was a big amount - and told my father: 'This will be his monthly salary from today.' It was embarrassing for my father to say 'yes' to him! That's how my long cricket career began," recollected former India stumper Chandrakant Pandit, while fighting his emotions as he spoke to TOI about the most loved coach on the cricket maidans of Mumbai.                The news of Achrekar's demise saw a pall of gloom descend on Mumbai's cricket community. Even as they rushed to have a last glimpse of their beloved 'sir', several of his students, who went on to play for Mumbai, India, or at least bagged a job because of the cricket skills that he had imparted, paid rich tributes to a man who played a much bigger role in their life than just being a cricket coach.                "Personally, it's a big loss for me. He made me the person I am. He taught me how to deal with hard times on and off the field. His love for the game was real," said ex-India batsman-turned coach Pravin Amre.                "The service he has given to not only Mumbai cricket but to even world cricket is unparalleled. I hope Sachin (Tendulkar) takes it well," said Sandeep Patil.                "All his memories are flashing in front of me...of sir walking in to Shivaji Park. It was only about seven-eight days back when I was telling my wife and daughter that his presence at Shivaji Park was like that of a lion. It's a big loss for Mumbai and Indian cricket," said former Mumbai stalwart Amol Muzumdar.                "He was like a father-figure and mentor to me. He was the one who spotted my talent. I never played inter-school cricket - Harris or Giles Shield. I just went to a summer vacation camp in RCF, since I stayed in BARC which was nearby. He picked me up there, and then he was after me to change my school. When I was in standard XI, he got me admission in Ruia College," said former India opener and coach Lalchand Rajput.                "I started my journey under Achrekar sir. I owe everything to him. He instilled one value in me early on: 'you need to practise, and you need to work hard'. He made sure that in our initial years we practised and played a lot of matches. That laid a solid foundation for us to build our cricket on," remembered former India pacer Paras Mhambrey, currently the India A bowling coach.                "He is responsible for making the lives of so many cricketers, a cause for which he spent his entire life. And he had no 'selfish interest' behind it! I had no clue that I had any talent or potential. He gave a direction to our careers. It's not just that he produced Test or Ranji cricketers - but he helped people get jobs because of cricket. And that helped those people support their families. He's played a huge role in everyone's career," praised former India seamer Balwinder Singh Sandhu.    SOME FAMOUS ACHREKAR ANECDOTES          When Tendulkar and Kambli got a dressing down                The Test versus England in February 1993 at Wankhede is remembered for India inflicting an innings defeat that sealed the 3-0 whitewash. But what it is also famous for is the fact that the city's most fertile cricketing stable, Shardashram Vidyamandir, had three of its ex-students, in the playing XI. Tendulkar, Vinod Kambli and Amre. However, as the cameras looked for Achrekar in the crowd, that search proved to be futile. Achrekar was at the Cross Maidan as both his schools, Shardashram English and Marathi, were contesting the final of the Harris shield.                However, he was still up to date with the proceedings across the street and saw a few overs where he witnessed Tendulkar getting out for 78 and Kambli fishing at wide deliveries from Chris Lewis and Paul Jarvis. Both were summoned to the Cross Maidan and were given a dressing down. Achrekar asked Kambli, "Don't you know where your off-stump is?". The lecture helped Kambli as he went on to score 224 on Day 4.                The coins of motivation                At the Shardashram nets, when dusk almost married the evening, Achrekar used to give his favourite ward, Sachin Tendulkar, a new challenge. In fading light, after the 13-year-old was exhausted facing bowlers for almost an hour, he used to place a one rupee coin on top of the off-stump and tell the bowlers, I will give it to anyone who gets Sachin out. It was a challenge that most bowlers failed and the proof of that is the number of those coins that Sachin has in his treasure. That they still occupy pride of place in his opulent trophy cabinet, shows how stiff the challenge was and how expertly Tendulkar dealt with it.                Slap after a triple ton                Chandrakant Pandit scored a triple hundred in his very first match for Shardashram, and that too on the opening day of the game. However, Pandit was out after hitting the ball in the air. As soon as he came back, he was expecting that 'sir' would give him a pat on the back. However, Achrekar slapped him hard, leaving Pandit a little shocked. The boy realised his mistake when Achrekar told him that he could have batted on. Later, he slapped Pandit again, when he was caught brilliantly at covers while driving the ball, in a friendly game. Achrekar was upset with Pandit that he gave that chance to the fielder. He didn't want his students to come up with any excuses.
